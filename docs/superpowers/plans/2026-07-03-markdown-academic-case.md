## 1. The document-engineering tradition

**Claim:** A formal, decades-old strand of computer science argues that separating a document's logical structure from its visual presentation is not a stylistic preference but a structural requirement for consistency, reuse, and correctness at scale.

**Sources:**
- Brian K. Reid, *Scribe: A Document Specification Language and its Compiler*, PhD thesis, Carnegie Mellon University, 1980 (CMU-CS-81-100, advised by Robert Sproull). Scribe was the first system to cleanly separate document structure (what a passage *is*) from a separately maintained format database (how it *looks*), a design that directly influenced GML/SGML, LaTeX, and HTML. The work won Reid the ACM Grace Murray Hopper Award in 1982.
- Charles Goldfarb, Edward Mosher, and Raymond Lorie developed Generalized Markup Language (GML) at IBM in 1969; Goldfarb formalized it as the Standard Generalized Markup Language, standardized as ISO 8879:1986. SGML's founding premise was descriptive markup: tag what a passage *is* (a heading, a citation) rather than how it should render, so the same source serves multiple outputs and survives changes in typesetting technology.
- James H. Coombs, Allen H. Renear, and Steven J. DeRose, "Markup Systems and the Future of Scholarly Text Processing," *Communications of the ACM* 30(11), 1987, pp. 933-947. This is the field's clearest formal argument: procedural and presentational markup (formatting codes, WYSIWYG styling) "retard the movement toward systems supporting scholars' thinking and writing," while descriptive markup "simplif[ies] mechanical tasks and allow[s] authors to focus their attention on the content." It is the most direct peer-reviewed statement of the claim in thread 1.
- Donald Knuth built TeX starting in 1977-78 specifically so authors could encode logical intent ("this is a keyword," "this is a display equation") rather than manually specifying ink positions; see Knuth, "The Errors of TeX," *Software: Practice and Experience* 19(7), 1989, pp. 607-685, a ten-year log of TeX's evolution that documents the engineering discipline behind declarative typesetting.

**Caveat:** This is engineering and design-theory argument, developed and defended by its own practitioners, not an empirical finding about outcomes. No controlled study measures whether SGML/TeX-style documents are more consistent or reusable than WYSIWYG-authored ones in practice; the case rests on formal reasoning about what descriptive versus procedural markup structurally permits.

## 2. The reproducible-research movement

**Claim:** Computational and quantitative academia converged on plain-text authoring (Markdown/RMarkdown/LaTeX plus Pandoc) as the substrate for reproducible work, because plain text integrates cleanly with version control and with automated document generation from code.

**Sources:**
- Kieran Healy (Professor of Sociology, Duke University), *The Plain Person's Guide to Plain Text Social Science* (this version 2020-10-24; earlier as a widely circulated web essay from 2018, plain-text.co). Healy frames the choice as the "Office model" (Word file as the single real artifact, changes tracked inside it) versus the "Engineering model" (plain text files as the real artifact, changes tracked by an external version-control system, output generated via Pandoc). He is explicit that this is not dogma: "There is little to be gained from plain-text dogmatism in a .docx world," and he includes a dissenting practitioner's view (pseudonymous engineer "Dr. Drang") skeptical of distraction-free-editor claims. Healy's guide is a practitioner methods essay, not peer-reviewed, but is a standard citation in computational social science pedagogy.
- Greg Wilson, Jennifer Bryan, Karen Cranston, Justin Kitzes, Lex Nederbragt, and Tracy K. Teal, "Good Enough Practices in Scientific Computing," *PLOS Computational Biology* 13(6), 2017, e1005510. Peer-reviewed and widely cited; Recommendation 6b explicitly advises writing manuscripts "in a plain text format that permits version control, such as LaTeX or Markdown," converting to PDF/HTML with Pandoc, because version control "is optimized for plain text files" and "the ability to see so-called 'diffs' is one of the great joys of version control," whereas with binary formats "it is not possible to pinpoint specific changes from 1 version to the next."
- Ben Baumer, Mine Cetinkaya-Rundel, Andrew Bray, Linda Loi, and Nicholas J. Horton, "R Markdown: Integrating a Reproducible Analysis Tool into Introductory Statistics," *Technology Innovations in Statistics Education* 8(1), 2014. Peer-reviewed pedagogical case for interleaving prose and code in one plain-text source so the rendered document and the underlying computation cannot drift apart.
- The lineage traces to Knuth's literate programming, introduced in "Literate Programming," *The Computer Journal* 27(2), 1984: "instead of imagining that our main task is to instruct a computer what to do, let us concentrate rather on explaining to human beings what we want the computer to do." This is the conceptual ancestor of RMarkdown/Jupyter's mixed code-and-prose plain-text documents.

**Caveat:** The strongest evidence here is disciplinary consensus and adoption pattern (a large fraction of computational social science, statistics, and bioinformatics literature now ships as RMarkdown/Jupyter/Pandoc source), backed by one peer-reviewed methods paper (Wilson et al.) that makes the plain-text argument as a recommendation rather than reporting a comparative outcome study. No randomized or controlled study demonstrates that plain-text authoring produces more reproducible papers than Word-based workflows; the claim is inferred from tooling logic (version control needs line-oriented diffs; code-and-prose interleaving needs a parseable plain-text format) rather than measured.

## 3. Writing-process cognition

**Claim:** Formatting decisions made during composition draw on the same limited attention that composition itself requires, so deferring formatting should reduce interruption. This is the weakest-evidenced thread; say so plainly.

**Sources:**
- Linda Flower and John R. Hayes, "A Cognitive Process Theory of Writing," *College Composition and Communication* 32(4), 1981, pp. 365-387. Foundational (via think-aloud protocol analysis) in showing that writing is not linear but a recursive competition among planning, translating, and reviewing processes, and that "generate" and "evaluate" sub-processes can interrupt composition at any point, forcing goal revision. This establishes that composition is interruption-sensitive in general; it says nothing specific about formatting or WYSIWYG interfaces, since word processors did not yet exist as studied objects in 1981.
- Thomas R. G. Green and Marian Petre, "Usability Analysis of Visual Programming Environments: A 'Cognitive Dimensions' Framework," *Journal of Visual Languages and Computing* 7, 1996, pp. 131-174. This HCI framework, not a writing study, formalizes "premature commitment" (a notation forcing decisions before the information needed to make them is available) and "viscosity" (resistance of a notation to local change) as named costs of a notation's design. The framework has been explicitly applied to word-processor style sheets: a style-sheet abstraction reduces viscosity relative to per-instance formatting, because changing the style updates every instance at once. This is the closest formal vocabulary available for why hardcoded, in-place formatting decisions (as WYSIWYG editors invite) impose a cost that a deferred, declarative style layer (as Markdown plus a stylesheet/renderer) avoids.
- Christina Haas, *Writing Technology: Studies on the Materiality of Literacy*, Lawrence Erlbaum Associates, 1996, examines empirically how writing technology shapes planning and revision behavior, but centers on paper-versus-screen and does not isolate WYSIWYG-versus-plain-text as a variable.
- Matthew Kirschenbaum, *Track Changes: A Literary History of Word Processing*, Harvard University Press, 2016, documents the historical reception of WYSIWYG word processing among professional writers (mixed: some authors deliberately chose plainer, typewriter-like interfaces such as Courier-only display over rich formatting), but this is literary-historical and anecdotal rather than an empirical finding.

**Caveat, stated directly:** No controlled study directly compares WYSIWYG and plain-text/structured writing environments for writer fluency, focus, or output quality. The case for thread 3 is an analytic extrapolation: general writing-process research (Flower and Hayes) establishes that composition is attention-limited and interruption-sensitive, and a separate HCI framework (Green and Petre) formally names the cost that in-place formatting choices impose, but no study links these two literatures directly to WYSIWYG-versus-Markdown. Healy's own guide, sourcing a skeptical practitioner, explicitly warns against overstating distraction-free-editor claims. A documentation section should present this thread as the weakest of the five and frame it as engineering judgment informed by adjacent research, not as an empirical result.

## 4. Durability and preservation

**Claim:** Archival and preservation institutions state, as settled practice, that open, simple, self-documenting formats without proprietary dependencies carry materially lower long-term preservation risk than complex proprietary or vendor-specific binary formats.

**Sources:**
- Library of Congress, *Sustainability of Digital Formats: Planning for Library of Congress Collections* (loc.gov/preservation/digital/formats/) and *Recommended Formats Statement* (updated annually; 2025-2026 edition at loc.gov/preservation/resources/rfs/). The Library's sustainability factors are explicit: disclosure, adoption, transparency, self-documentation, freedom from external dependencies, absence of patent encumbrance, and absence of technical protection mechanisms. Formats scoring well on these criteria are structurally simpler and less dependent on a single vendor's software stack.
- Digital Preservation Coalition, *Digital Preservation Handbook*, "File Formats and Standards" (dpconline.org/handbook). States the field consensus directly: preferred formats should be "open, standard, non-proprietary, and well-established."
- General digital-preservation literature on format obsolescence (Wikipedia's "Digital obsolescence" entry summarizes the consensus, itself drawing on Smithsonian Institution Archives and UTSA Libraries preservation-framework documentation): risk of inaccessibility rises with dependency on specific hardware or software to interpret a format, and "formats proprietary to one software vendor are more likely to be affected by format obsolescence."

**Caveat:** Modern `.docx` (Office Open XML) is itself an ISO/ECMA standard (ISO/IEC 29500), not a secret proprietary format, so the plain-text-versus-proprietary framing needs precision: the Library of Congress's own criteria (self-documentation, simplicity, absence of external dependencies) still favor a bare UTF-8 text file over a compressed, multi-part XML container with embedded objects, complex rendering dependencies, and versioned feature sets, but this is a difference of degree (simplicity and dependency surface) rather than the older, starker open-versus-proprietary framing the case sometimes implies. No institution states "Markdown is a Library of Congress preferred format" outright, from what could be verified; the applicable and citable claim is the general criteria, applied to the comparison.

## 5. Versionability

**Claim:** Line-oriented plain text is the input format that line-based diff algorithms, and by extension the entire git/version-control review workflow, were built to operate on; prose stored any other way loses meaningful diffing.

**Sources:**
- James W. Hunt and M. Douglas McIlroy, "An Algorithm for Differential File Comparison," Bell Laboratories Computing Science Technical Report #41, June 1976. The foundational diff algorithm (a longest-common-subsequence solution) that Unix `diff`, and downstream every git-style diff, is built on. It is defined over sequences of lines; there is no equivalent line-level structure in a binary or richly nested document format.
- Wilson et al. 2017 (cited above, thread 2) states the consequence for manuscripts directly: version control "is optimized for plain text files," and with a binary format such as a Word document "it is not possible to pinpoint specific changes from 1 version to the next," whereas plain text yields diffs that isolate the exact changed lines.
- Healy's guide (cited above) frames this as the core practical distinction between the Office model, where "changes to your work are tracked *inside* that file," typically via Word's Track Changes, and the Engineering model, where "changes are tracked outside of files" by a version-control system operating on the plain-text source, enabling branching, merging, and per-change attribution across collaborators.

**Caveat:** This claim is closer to settled engineering fact than the other four: it follows directly from how the diff algorithm is defined (line-oriented, textual) and is not contested in the literature surveyed. The scholarly treatment is practitioner and methods literature (Healy, Wilson et al.) rather than a dedicated peer-reviewed study of diff quality for prose specifically, but the underlying algorithmic claim (Hunt-McIlroy) is uncontroversial computer science.

## Synthesis (the case that survives scrutiny, flat, no advocacy)

Document engineering has argued since Reid's 1980 Scribe thesis and Goldfarb's SGML that separating a text's logical structure from its visual presentation makes documents more consistent and reusable, a position given its clearest formal statement in Coombs, Renear, and DeRose's 1987 CACM paper contrasting descriptive with procedural markup. Computational and quantitative academia has, in practice, converged on plain-text authoring (RMarkdown, Pandoc, literate programming's Knuth-derived lineage) because it integrates directly with version control and with automated generation of output from code, a case argued explicitly in Healy's practitioner guide and made with peer-reviewed backing in Wilson et al.'s "Good Enough Practices in Scientific Computing." The cognitive case that formatting decisions interrupt composition is the weakest of the five: Flower and Hayes establish that writing is attention-limited and interruption-sensitive in general, and Green and Petre's cognitive-dimensions framework formally names the cost ("premature commitment," "viscosity") that in-place formatting choices impose, but no study directly measures WYSIWYG against plain text for writer fluency, so this thread is analytic inference, not empirical finding. Preservation institutions, chiefly the Library of Congress's stated sustainability criteria, prefer formats that are simple, self-documenting, and free of vendor and rendering dependencies, a criterion plain text satisfies more fully than even standardized formats like Office Open XML, though the comparison is one of degree, not open-versus-secret. Line-oriented plain text is the one claim resting on uncontested computer science: the Hunt-McIlroy diff algorithm underlying git operates on lines of text, so plain-text prose is the one authoring format for which "what changed" has an exact, automatically computable answer.
