The calibration candidates

Live reproductions in the site’s own tokens (slate ink, the blue accent, the system stack — all kept per the brief). Two decisions: how quiet the home page’s top becomes, and which density treatment the education schedule gets. Real copy throughout, trimmed where noted.

Decision A — the home page’s top

The current treatment stacks three chrome layers. The revised candidate keeps the section-band device (ratified: it works — wayfinding without chrome) and removes only the boxing: the welcome becomes typography on the white ground, the notification a slim accent strip, and the news cards sit directly on their band. The rule: bands mark sections, cards mark objects, nothing gets both. A consistent spacing scale replaces the ad-hoc gaps.

Decision B — the education schedule’s density

The real copy, three ways. B0 is today’s wall. B1 paces it with editorial devices only (an at-a-glance table, then subheaded prose). B2 adds the selective card — one quiet card per day — the rhythm device, not the chrome habit.

Arrival

You’re welcome to arrive any time before the class, and many students opt to come out the day before to get settled. If you do this, you may find ASC volunteers prepping and rigging boats for the class. You’re welcome to join in; we always appreciate the help…

Day one (Thursday), 1:00–6:00 p.m.

Class starts later on Thursday, so our instructors have time to prepare boats and materials on Thursday morning. We’ll start the class at 1:00 p.m. with introductions and then take a brief walking tour of the club. After that, we’ll jump right into hands-on rigging… and do the dreaded (but fun!) capsize drill. Once everybody has had a chance to dry off, we’ll have a brief classroom session to cover basic theory, the mechanics of sailing, and basic maneuvers…

Day two (Friday), 10:00 a.m.–6:00 p.m.

We’ll spend as much time on the water as the wind allows during the second day. We’ll begin the day by getting the boats rigged and launched. From there, we’ll do a brief classroom review and then head to the lake for our first drill…

Arrival

You’re welcome to arrive any time before the class, and many students come out the day before to get settled. Early arrivals may find volunteers prepping boats — join in; it’s a head start on the rigging we’ll do in class.

Day one — Thursday, 1:00–6:00 p.m.

Introductions and a walking tour, then straight into hands-on rigging — learning the parts and pieces as we go. Launch, then the dreaded (but fun!) capsize drill. After everyone dries off: basic theory, tacking and jibing, dry-land tiller practice, and a Q&A to close.

Day two — Friday, 10:00 a.m.–6:00 p.m.

As much water time as the wind allows: rig, launch, review, then the first drill — beam reaching and tacking around two marks. Upwind and downwind sailing fills the afternoon; it’s the hardest part for beginners, so we stay with it.

(Saturday and Sunday continue in the same shape.)

The color story — “the club grounds”

The current palette is Blowfish’s defaults; the club’s own materials are richer. The story: flag navy for identity, gold for the marks, fireweed for the one pop, the buildings’ sage in the neutrals — and the purple stays on the doors (declining a hue is what makes five materials a story instead of a paint box). Semantic colors remain a separate reserved vocabulary.

What happens with your picks

A-pick and B-pick become locked design recipes in the spec: A calibrates the chrome-quieting across every page top and band; B becomes the density toolkit’s house style for long content (education, the guides, racing). The MembershipWorks hand-off stays as-is per the known constraint. Then the spec ratifies and the build plan gets written.
