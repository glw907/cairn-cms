We got to the trailhead forty minutes later than planned because the shuttle driver took a wrong turn past the reservoir and had to loop back through the campground, and by the time we shouldered our packs the morning fog still had not burned off the lower switchbacks, so the first hour was cool and grey and quiet except for somebody's trekking poles clicking against the granite; Marcus called out the first marmot sighting around mile two, a fat one sunning itself on a boulder just off the trail, and we stopped long enough for three people to take the same photo before pushing on toward the saddle, where the wind picked up hard enough that Priya's hat went sailing into a patch of scrub willow and took two of us ten minutes to retrieve; lunch was at the tarn below the ridge, cold couscous and summer sausage and a bag of dried apricots that somebody had clearly packed in 2024 judging by the texture, and the light was good enough that nobody complained; the climb out of the basin was the hard part, loose scree for a quarter mile and then a boulder field where Deb rolled her ankle just enough to slow the group but not enough to turn us around, so we taped it at the next flat spot and kept moving at a gentler pace; by the time we reached base camp the wind had died and the sky had gone that flat pewter color that usually means rain by morning, which it did, a steady drizzle starting around four that sent everyone into their tents earlier than the itinerary called for; dinner was cooked half in the vestibules, freeze-dried chili and instant rice, and somebody's stove flared badly enough to singe a guyline, which we will be hearing about for weeks; the rain let up around midnight and the stars came out hard and close the way they do above nine thousand feet, and at least two people were up at two in the morning just to look at them before crawling back into damp sleeping bags for a few more hours; the walk out the next day was uneventful in the way a good trip always ends, quiet talk, a long snack break at the tarn again, and the shuttle actually on time for once, though nobody will believe that part until it happens twice.
